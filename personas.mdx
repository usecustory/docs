Use personas when the same journey means different things to different customer roles and your team needs to stay clear about whose problem it is actually solving.

In Custory, they are workspace-level operating assets, not a separate documentation exercise.

## How personas change decisions

A persona is a reusable customer profile that helps your team interpret a journey through the right customer lens.

Use personas to answer questions like:

- Who is this journey really for?
- Is this problem affecting the buyer, the admin, or the end user?
- Would this solution help the right customer?

Without personas, teams often talk about "the user" as if every customer has the same goals, constraints, and buying role.

That breaks down quickly in B2B products and lean SaaS teams where one person evaluates, another sets up, and another uses the product day to day.

## When personas sharpen the map

Create a persona when a different role, workflow, or decision pattern changes how the journey should be interpreted.

Do not create a new persona for every minor variation.

## How personas work in Custory

Personas live at the workspace level so they can be reused across journeys.

That avoids a common failure mode where each map creates a slightly different version of the same customer profile.

Personas become most valuable when linked to the journeys where they actually shape interpretation.

## What to include

Custory supports detailed persona editing across practical fields such as:

- bio
- role
- role in journey
- primary job-to-be-done
- buying influence
- budget or income
- location
- goals
- frustrations
- motivations
- tech savviness
- devices
- tools and apps

Use these fields selectively. A sharp useful persona is better than a complete but stale one.

## Good persona examples

### First-time workspace admin

This person wants fast setup, low risk, and enough clarity to become the internal champion.

Good fields:

- Role: operations manager
- Role in journey: team admin
- Goal: get the team to first value without engineering help
- Frustration: too many setup choices too early

### Founder evaluating ROI

This person wants confidence that the product is worth team time and budget.

Good fields:

- Role: founder
- Role in journey: buyer or approver
- Goal: see practical value fast
- Frustration: tools that create more process than clarity

## Link personas to journeys

Link an existing persona when a journey clearly serves that customer type.

Create a new persona from the journey when you discover a distinct customer role during mapping.

Unlink a persona when it no longer changes how the team should interpret that journey.

## AI support for personas

Custory AI can help with persona work by:

- drafting a new persona
- tightening generic language
- expanding goals, frustrations, and motivations
- suggesting which journeys a persona should be linked to

## Persona mistakes to avoid

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Writing personas like a brand deck">
    If the persona sounds polished but does not change product decisions, it is too abstract. Write for operating clarity, not for presentation quality.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Duplicating minor variations">
    Create a separate persona only when the role, workflow, or decision logic is meaningfully different. Otherwise the team ends up maintaining several versions of the same customer.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Filling every field because it exists">
    Only keep the fields that sharpen decisions for your team. Extra detail is useful only when someone will actually use it during review, prioritization, or design.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## What a useful persona looks like

A good persona helps the team:

- interpret evidence more accurately
- frame opportunities for the right customer
- avoid solving the wrong problem for the wrong role
