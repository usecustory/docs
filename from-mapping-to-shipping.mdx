Use this guide when your journey is mapped well enough to support decisions and you now need to turn that map into shipped improvements.

## When to stop mapping and start acting

Do not wait for a perfect journey.

Start acting when you can answer five basic questions:

- what part of the journey the problem belongs to
- what evidence supports it
- who it affects
- what problem is worth solving first
- what metric could tell you if progress happened

If those answers are visible, the journey is already useful enough to move from mapping into action.

## What changes after the map exists

Mapping helps your team understand the customer experience.

Shipping starts when you use that understanding to choose one problem, assign ownership, and push follow-through into the tools and workflows the team already uses.

The practical shift is:

- from collecting observations
- to framing one opportunity clearly
- to choosing a response
- to tracking whether the response helped

## Pick the first opportunity worth pursuing

Choose the first opportunity that is both meaningful and usable.

Good signs:

- the evidence appears more than once
- the customer moment is specific
- the problem affects an important step in the journey
- the team could realistically respond this week or this sprint

Avoid starting with the biggest possible theme.

Better:

- `Reduce confusion before the Slack connection step`
- `Shorten time to first mapped journey`

Worse:

- `Fix onboarding`
- `Improve activation`

## Assign one clear owner

Once an opportunity is real, give it an owner.

The owner is the person responsible for follow-through, not the only person who will touch the work.

For a lean team, that usually means:

- a product lead or founder for prioritization
- an engineer or designer for the response
- a customer-facing teammate when the fix depends on support, onboarding, or docs

If an item matters enough to discuss repeatedly, it matters enough to have an owner.

## Turn the opportunity into linked work

Use the journey to keep the reasoning visible while work moves forward.

The usual path is:

1. capture the [opportunity](/opportunities)
2. add one or more candidate [solutions](/solutions)
3. link the solution to the opportunity
4. connect the work to [external tasks](/external-tasks) or your delivery tool
5. keep the delivery work attached to the original customer context

This prevents the common failure mode where a team creates tickets but loses the customer reason behind them.

## Decide what metric will tell you if it worked

Before the work ships, define the number that matters.

That metric should answer one of two questions:

- is this problem big enough to act on
- did the response improve the customer experience

Examples:

- setup completion rate
- median time to first value
- invite completion rate
- trial-to-paid conversion

If possible, add the baseline first. That makes later review faster and less emotional.

## Worked example

Here is what the full shift can look like in practice:

- Touchpoint: customer reaches the invite teammate step
- Insight: solo admins feel pushed to invite others before they are ready
- Opportunity: reduce pressure at the invite teammate step
- Solution: add a skip-and-return-later path and rewrite the helper copy
- External task: linked product task for the onboarding flow update
- Metric: invite completion rate within 3 days and first-session completion rate

The map explains why the team is acting. The linked work moves the change forward. The metric tells you whether it helped.

## Review the result, not just the output

Once a solution ships:

1. return to the original step in the journey
2. review the linked metric
3. check for fresh signals from support, analytics, or feedback
4. update the item statuses
5. decide whether the problem is improving, needs another response, or should be deprioritized

That loop is what keeps Custory from becoming a map you visit once and abandon.

## Mistakes to avoid

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Waiting for a complete map before taking action">
    A focused journey with strong evidence is usually enough to start. Completeness is less important than clarity.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Jumping straight from insight to ticket">
    Keep the opportunity visible first so the team stays aligned on the problem, not just the first proposed fix.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Shipping without a success measure">
    If nobody defines the metric up front, later reviews turn into opinion instead of learning.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## A strong handoff looks like this

A strong mapping-to-shipping workflow lets your team say:

- this is the customer moment we are improving
- this is the evidence behind it
- this is the problem we chose
- this is the response we shipped
- this is how we know whether it worked
